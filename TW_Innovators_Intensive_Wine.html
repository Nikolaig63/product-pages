Why Wine

An industry at
a deflection point.

The wine industry is not the point. It is the laboratory. The point is this: every industry that has been growing confidently has been building on assumptions that feel like facts—because growth confirmed them. When the environment shifts, those assumptions don’t fail gradually. They fail in clusters, at the worst possible moment. Wine is where that pattern is visible. Participants work on it in Provence—in the vineyards and cooperatives where the assumptions are embedded—because the pattern they see here is the same pattern that exists inside every organisation they lead.

Think Wrong is most powerful when the problem is genuinely uncertain. The future of wine is not a case study—it is a live industry challenge with real stakeholders, real constraints, and no obvious right answer. Participants work on it in the place where it is most viscerally felt.

01

Climate is redrawing the map

The terroirs that defined the category for centuries are shifting. New regions are emerging. Established appellations face existential decisions about identity versus adaptation. The assumptions that built the industry are becoming liabilities.

02

The next generation is not drinking wine

Consumption among 21–34-year-olds is declining across every major market. The beverage landscape has fragmented into hundreds of new choices competing for the occasions wine once owned. The distribution and attention models that sustained the industry are breaking down.

03

Complexity is a barrier, not a feature

The expertise required to navigate the wine category—appellations, varietals, vintages, producers—is experienced by most consumers as exclusion, not invitation. The industry has optimized for connoisseurs and is losing everyone else.

04

Consolidation is changing what the industry is for

Investment groups and corporate négociants are acquiring family domaines across Provence, Burgundy, and the Rhône at an accelerating pace. The model that replaces them is more efficient, more consistent, and more heavily marketed—and it is built on entirely different assumptions about what makes a wine worth drinking. The small and medium family producers who defined the category’s identity are facing an economic logic that their craft cannot easily argue with. The question is not whether consolidation will continue. It is whether the assumptions that made family wine meaningful can survive it—and what a different economic model for small producers might look like.

“The wine industry’s most dangerous assumption is that quality—as the family producers have always defined it—is a sufficient answer to the economics that are buying them out.”

Frame the right problem before committing to a solution.

You apply it to the wine industry on Monday and to your own organisation on Friday.

What the week produces

The pattern behind
the challenge.

The wine industry is the laboratory precisely because its governance failure is visible. The domaines under the most pressure—from consolidation, from a generation that drinks differently, from the slow erosion of the heritage narrative—are not failing at wine-making. They built their value in one place while the forces that determine whether that value lasts drifted without anyone measuring them. The community that used to inherit the habit stopped inheriting it. The political capital sustaining appellation protection weakened. The financial results moved last—a symptom of everything that had already shifted upstream. The week in Provence is designed to make that pattern legible. Not because you are in the wine industry. But because the same pattern—building confidently in one direction while other forms of value quietly erode—appears in every organisation that measures only its financial results while the forces that precede them go ungoverned. By Day 4, most participants can name at least one assumption in their own organisation that belongs to the same category. For those who can, the next conversation is about whether that assumption warrants a more structured look. That is what the diagnostic is for.

The vineyards don’t teach you about wine. They teach you to see the assumptions your own organisation has stopped questioning.

If that pattern sounds familiar before you’ve even attended—the conversation starts here.

In the Field

The vineyards
are the classroom.

Each fieldwork excursion is a structured learning session with specific Think Wrong frameworks to apply, specific questions to bring, and specific outputs to produce. The wine industry is the curriculum. Provence is the campus.

Domaine interviews

Structured conversations with vignerons using past-behavior anchoring. Not “what do you think about the future?” but “tell me about the last time a decision you made turned out to be wrong.” Stories, not opinions.

Cave and cooperative visits

Immersive visits to the production infrastructure—caves where wine is aged, cooperatives where small producers share resources, cold-chain facilities where the economics of distribution become visible and tangible.

Consumer fieldwork

Structured interviews with wine consumers and non-consumers at market, in restaurants, and in retail. Lean CD methodology applied live—the goal is stories about past behavior, not opinions about future intent.

New model encounters

Visits to producers who have broken the category rules—biodynamic, natural wine, no-label, direct-to-consumer. These are the edge cases where new assumptions about what wine can be are being tested in real conditions.

Immersive tastings as research

Guided tastings are research sessions, not entertainment. Participants use the Certainty Map to classify what they can sense, what they can learn, and what the industry assumes the consumer can understand.

Stakeholder panel

Teams present their small bets to a curated panel of industry stakeholders. Real feedback from real people with skin in the game. The most valuable session of the week—because the signal is unfiltered.

What You Learn

The Think Wrong
methodology—applied.

Every tool is introduced in the classroom and immediately applied in the field. By Day 4, participants are facilitating their peers through the tools they learned on Day 1.

1

Deflection Point—framing the right problem

The from/to shift that defines the challenge. Applied to the wine industry’s existential tensions on Day 1—and to participants’ own organizational challenges on Day 4.5. The most important innovation question is not “what should we build?”—it is “are we working on the right problem?”

2

Certainty Map—mapping what is known, unknown, and learnable

Every innovation challenge contains beliefs masquerading as facts. The Certainty Map surfaces the distinction and identifies what would need to be true for the team’s hypotheses to be correct. Applied to the wine consumer challenge on Day 2.

3

PAK + Super Vital Assumptions—testing what matters first

Pain, Assumption, Knowledge. The SVA Drill prioritizes assumptions by certainty and impact—identifying which are Tackle First (low certainty, high impact) and must be tested before capital is consumed. The discipline that prevents innovation from burning money on the wrong bets.

4

Make Stuff + Bet Small—building to learn

Small bets that convert uncertainty into learning. Tangible, testable artifacts put in front of real stakeholders the same day they are made. Build to learn, not to present. Evidence before investment.

5

The Next Cycle—advancing innovation systematically

The iterative framework that holds all the tools together. Participants leave able to design and facilitate a full Next Cycle sprint in their own organization—using the wine industry as the reference experience that makes the framework concrete and memorable.
