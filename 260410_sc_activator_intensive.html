Why Think Wrong

The bets that matter most are the ones you can’t afford to get wrong.

Every significant growth initiative has a moment—usually early, usually brief—when the assumptions underneath it are still examinable. When the team hasn’t yet committed. When the timeline hasn’t yet collapsed the space for honest inquiry. When someone could still ask the question that needs to be asked. Think Wrong is the discipline for that moment. It is not a creativity methodology or an idea generation tool. It is the structured practice for surfacing what your organisation believes, distinguishing it from what your organisation actually knows, and designing the smallest action that converts belief into evidence—before the capital commits and the window closes.

The result is that capital is committed before the most consequential assumptions are tested. When the environment shifts—and it always does—the assumptions don’t fail gradually. They fail in clusters, at the worst possible moment, when the capital has already been deployed and reversing the commitment carries a cost that makes reversal feel reckless. The Value Building Activator Intensive is the discipline that interrupts this pattern—before the commitment is made.

01

The confidence in the room is not the same as evidence.

Most significant growth bets feel certain from the inside. The team believes in the initiative. The strategic logic is coherent. The numbers support the narrative. None of this is evidence. Think Wrong is the practice that makes the distinction—and finds the question that hasn’t been asked yet.

02

The most dangerous moment is when execution replaces inquiry.

Organisations with strong execution capability are particularly vulnerable. Once the decision is made and the team mobilises, asking whether the problem was correctly framed feels like obstruction. Think Wrong creates the discipline to stay in the question longer—before the momentum makes the question unwelcome.

03

The timeline always arrives before the evidence does.

The pressure to commit—from stakeholders, boards, competitive dynamics, and organisational momentum—consistently outpaces the pace at which evidence can be gathered. Think Wrong doesn’t slow the timeline. It designs evidence-generating actions that fit inside it—small, credible, fast enough to matter before the window closes.

04

The people building the capital are the last to question the bet.

Stopping is treated as failure. Questioning is treated as obstruction. The same forces that make an organisation good at execution make it resistant to the honest inquiry that would have prevented the expensive mistake. Think Wrong gives leaders the practice and the permission to ask the question the room isn’t asking—without it reading as a lack of conviction.

“The most expensive question in any organisation is the one no one asked before the capital committed.”

What goes home with you.

The Activator Intensive develops the same four core Think Wrong capabilities as the field-based Innovators Intensives—applied directly to your own live organizational challenge rather than to an external industry. Three days. Your challenge. Real stakes. By Day 3, most participants can run these practices with their own teams.

01

Frame the right problem before committing to a solution.

Most growth initiatives fail not because the team didn’t work hard enough, but because they were working on the wrong problem. This practice gives you a structured way to step back, find the real challenge, and redirect effort before budget commits. The difference between a product that drives revenue and one that misses the market is almost always here.

02

Generate stronger ideas—and test them fast.

The obvious solutions to obvious problems are already being worked on by your competitors. This practice helps you generate ideas that wouldn’t emerge from a standard brainstorm—and build quick, low-cost tests that produce real signal before serious investment. Teams that do this consistently find better answers faster.

03

Find the two or three beliefs that could make or break the bet.

Not every assumption carries the same risk. This practice helps you identify the beliefs that, if wrong, would change the decision entirely—and design small, fast tests that reduce uncertainty before you’re committed. It’s the difference between a confident investment and an expensive regret.

04

Build the buy-in that actually moves things forward.

The best idea in the room doesn’t win. The idea with the most committed people behind it does. This practice gives you the facilitation tools to move a team from debate to shared conviction—so that when the decision is made, the people who need to execute it are already owners of it. This is what separates value builders from people who manage consensus.

01
Day One
Be Bold / Get Out & Get · Surface
Frame the right problem—before solutions take hold

Most organizations begin with solutions and work backward to the problem. The Deflection Point discipline reverses this: participants map the from/to transformation their challenge requires and ask whether the problem they are trying to solve is the actual cause of the fragility they need to address. Get Out & Get takes this into live fieldwork—structured consumer and stakeholder interviews using past-behavior anchoring, not opinion gathering. The goal is stories, not projections. By the end of Day 1, each participant has a reframed challenge statement and a first evidence-gap map.

02
Day Two
Let Go / Make Stuff · Classify and Prioritize
Distinguish what you know from what you believe—then test what matters most

The Certainty Map is the central evidence-based instrument of this programme: participants map every significant belief underlying their challenge as either untested assumption, testable hypothesis, or validated knowledge. The PAK framework—Presumptions (untestable), Assumptions (testable but not yet tested), Knowledge (evidenced)—makes the classification rigorous. The Super Vital Assumption discipline then prioritizes: which assumptions, if false, would change a investment decision that only the CFO or CEO can authorize? Make Stuff converts those assumptions into the smallest credible evidence-generating artifacts—prototypes, interview guides, test designs—that can produce real signal before capital is committed. By Day 2 close, each participant has a ranked assumption map and at least one small bet designed to test their highest-consequence assumption.

Day 3 opens with the escalation question: of all the assumptions surfaced across the three days, which ones, if they turned out to be wrong, would change a investment decision that only the CFO or CEO can authorize?
