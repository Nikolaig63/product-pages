Why Irish Whiskey

When growth makes
assumptions invisible.

Irish whiskey has grown at 12–15% annually for a decade. That growth has produced something that affects every fast-growing category: overconfidence that growth confirmed. Every new distillery believes premium positioning, terroir storytelling, and direct-to-consumer are defensible moats. Very few have tested whether those beliefs are actually true—or simply feel true because growth has never forced the question. The Irish whiskey challenge is not the point. It is the laboratory. The point is the pattern—and the discipline for testing assumptions before the environment does it for you. AI is compressing every investment cycle—the window between ‘we’re exploring this’ and ‘the budget is committed’ is closing faster than at any point in the last decade. The whiskey industry’s challenge—forty distilleries making the same confident bet on untested assumptions—mirrors the AI deployment decisions every senior leader is navigating right now. The pattern is identical. The cost of being wrong is the same.

Irish whiskey is not a category in crisis—it is a category in inflation. The assumptions built during growth are the most dangerous ones, because they have never been tested against adversity. The Innovators Intensive brings Think Wrong to the moment before the test arrives.

01

The growth assumptions have never been stress-tested

Irish whiskey grew on the back of a global super-premium spirits boom, a favorable comparison to Scotch, and a diaspora connection that made Irish identity a global commercial asset. All three tailwinds are moderating simultaneously. The assumptions built during the boom have never been tested against a headwind.

02

Every new distillery is making the same bets

Over 40 new distilleries have opened in Ireland in the last decade, most of them making remarkably similar bets: premium price point, heritage narrative, terroir story, visitor experience, direct-to-consumer sales. The market cannot absorb 40 premium heritage Irish whiskeys. The assumptions underlying most of them are not differentiated.

03

The next generation is approaching spirits differently

Under-30 spirits consumers are the most researched, most values-driven, and most health-conscious cohort in the category’s history. The assumptions about what a whiskey drinker is and why they drink—built on the boomer and Gen X experience—are not transferring cleanly to the generation that will determine the category’s next decade.

04

The Scotch comparison is a double-edged sword

Irish whiskey defined its position largely in contrast to Scotch—smoother, more accessible, more approachable. That contrast strategy worked brilliantly during Scotch’s prestige peak. It now anchors Irish whiskey to a frame that the most interesting new producers are trying to escape, and that the next generation of consumers does not find meaningful.

“Irish whiskey is the only spirits category growing at double digits while building on assumptions that no one has tried to break. That is not safety. That is a most important to test assumption waiting to be tested by the market.”

01

Frame the right problem before committing to a solution.

Most growth initiatives fail not because the team didn’t work hard enough, but because they were working on the wrong problem. This practice gives you a structured way to step back, find the real challenge, and redirect effort before budget commits. The difference between a product that drives revenue and one that misses the market is almost always here.

02

Generate stronger ideas—and test them fast.

The obvious solutions to obvious problems are already being worked on by your competitors. This practice helps you generate ideas that wouldn’t emerge from a standard brainstorm—and build quick, low-cost tests that produce real signal before serious investment. Teams that do this consistently find better answers faster.

03

Find the two or three beliefs that could make or break the bet.

Not every assumption carries the same risk. This practice helps you identify the beliefs that, if wrong, would change the decision entirely—and design small, fast tests that reduce uncertainty before you’re committed. It’s the difference between a confident investment and an expensive regret.

04

Build the buy-in that actually moves things forward.

The best idea in the room doesn’t win. The idea with the most committed people behind it does. This practice gives you the facilitation tools to move a team from debate to shared conviction—so that when the decision is made, the people who need to execute it are already owners of it. This is what separates value builders from people who manage consensus.

What the week produces

The pattern behind
the challenge.

The Irish whiskey industry is the laboratory precisely because its vulnerability is hidden in plain sight. The distilleries under the most risk are not failing at making whiskey. They built their value on a decade of double-digit growth—and in doing so, built an entire category on assumptions that growth never forced anyone to test. The next generation of drinkers is arriving on entirely different terms. The heritage narrative that justified premium pricing is becoming a liability with the consumers it most needs to attract. The forty-plus distilleries making the same bet have not yet encountered the adversity that will separate the ones with real foundations from the ones built on overconfidence that growth confirmed. The financial results have not yet reflected any of this. They rarely do until they must. The week on the Wild Atlantic Way is designed to make that pattern legible. Not because you are in the whiskey industry. But because the same pattern—growth masking the quiet erosion of the capital forms that determine whether growth lasts—appears in every organisation that has been building with confidence on assumptions it has never stress-tested. By Day 4, most participants can name at least one assumption in their own organisation that belongs to the same category. For those who can, the next conversation is about whether that assumption warrants a more structured look. That is what the diagnostic is for.

The pot still doesn’t teach you about whiskey. It teaches you to see the assumptions your own organisation has stopped questioning.

If that pattern sounds familiar before you’ve even attended—the conversation starts here.

What assumptions about the category has the market leader never needed to question? The answers to that question are the most commercially important signal of the week.

Craft distillery encounter

A smaller producer who has broken the premium heritage template—building on different assumptions about terroir, the consumer, and what Irish whiskey means to the next generation. Participants bring their SVA maps and test the craft assumptions against what they find in the still room and the tasting room.

On-trade consumer fieldwork

Structured interviews in pubs, whiskey bars, and hotel bars across the Wild Atlantic Way. Past-behavior anchored—what do current Irish whiskey drinkers remember about the first time they chose it? What do non-drinkers remember about why? The stories are the Certainty Map made real.

Landscape as method

The Wild Atlantic Way is not incidental to the programme—it is the medium. Walking sessions on the Connemara coast or the Burren are structured reflection sessions, not recreation. The landscape that shaped Irish identity—and by extension Irish whiskey—is the most visceral possible context for Deflection Point work.

Distribution and retail fieldwork

A structured visit to a specialist whiskey retailer and a conversation with a distributor about the on-trade and off-trade dynamics of the Irish whiskey boom. The distribution channel holds the most commercially sensitive assumptions in the value chain—and the most honest signal about where the growth is actually coming from.

Stakeholder panel

A distillery commercial director, an independent bottler, a whiskey retailer, a bartender who shapes on-trade consumption, and a consumer representing the next generation. Real small bets. Real feedback. The escalation question immediately follows. The most commercially consequential session in the programme portfolio.
