Why Surf

The discipline made
physical.

The surf industry is a perfect laboratory because its assumptions are visible in ways most industries’ assumptions are not. It built a global market on beliefs about authenticity, community, and the ocean—beliefs that growth confirmed for decades. Now those beliefs are colliding with climate, technology, and a new generation that experiences the ocean on entirely different terms. Participants don’t come to solve the surf industry’s problems. They come because the pattern the surf industry is living through is the same one that exists, at varying stages, inside every organisation they lead. The physical experience—in the water, in changing conditions—makes the discipline impossible to forget. AI is compressing every decision cycle—the window between ‘we’re exploring this’ and ‘the budget is locked’ is closing faster than at any point in the last decade. The surf industry’s challenge—strong execution capability applied to an incorrectly framed problem—shows up in every AI, growth, and transformation bet your organisation is making right now.

The deeper reason surf works as a Think Wrong laboratory is the methodology itself. Surfing is the most visceral possible expression of Lean Customer Development—you do not plan a ride, you read conditions, form a hypothesis, commit to a line, and learn immediately whether you were right. Every wipeout is a data point. Every session produces signal. The physical experience of the methodology reinforces the intellectual one in a way that no classroom setting can replicate.

01

Coaching has been commodified

The rise of surf tourism, certification programs, and beginner-focused schools has driven the price of instruction toward zero in the most competitive markets. The assumptions about the economic value of surf coaching—built when it was a scarce, locally embedded skill—are collapsing at speed as the supply of credentialed instructors grows faster than the market.

02

Destination competition has intensified

The Algarve, Morocco, the Canaries, the Azores, Bali, Sri Lanka, Nicaragua—all competing for the same European surf traveler. The geographic advantage that defined regional surf industries for decades is eroding as low-cost travel makes the world’s best breaks accessible to anyone prepared to research them.

03

Technology is disrupting the coaching model

AI video analysis, wave pools, surf simulators, and wearable performance data are restructuring the relationship between coach and learner. The assumption that physical presence in the water with an expert coach is the only effective way to learn is being challenged from multiple directions simultaneously.

04

The environmental assumptions are failing

Clean ocean, consistent swells, uncrowded breaks, accessible parking—the four conditions the surf industry was built on are all under pressure. Climate is altering swell patterns. Overcrowding has made the line-up at every major break a governance problem. The commons that the industry depended on is degrading.

“Every assumption in the surf industry was formed when the ocean was reliable, the market was growing, and the community governed itself. None of those conditions still apply everywhere.”

Four capabilities. One week.

What the ocean teaches.

The surf industry is the laboratory. The capabilities are what leave with you—and they are made physical here in a way that no classroom programme can replicate. Every framework is tested in conditions you did not choose, with practitioners who are living the problem. By Friday, you will have practised all four. Most participants are running sessions inside their own organisations within thirty days.

01

Frame the right problem before committing to a solution.

Most growth initiatives fail not because the team didn’t work hard enough, but because they were working on the wrong problem. This practice gives you a structured way to step back, find the real challenge, and redirect effort before budget commits. The difference between a product that drives revenue and one that misses the market is almost always here.

02

Generate stronger ideas—and test them fast.

The obvious solutions to obvious problems are already being worked on by your competitors. This practice helps you generate ideas that wouldn’t emerge from a standard brainstorm—and build quick, low-cost tests that produce real signal before serious investment. Teams that do this consistently find better answers faster.

03

Find the two or three beliefs that could make or break the bet.

Not every assumption carries the same risk. This practice helps you identify the beliefs that, if wrong, would change the decision entirely—and design small, fast tests that reduce uncertainty before you’re committed. It’s the difference between a confident investment and an expensive regret.

04

Build the buy-in that actually moves things forward.

The best idea in the room doesn’t win. The idea with the most committed people behind it does. This practice gives you the facilitation tools to move a team from debate to shared conviction—so that when the decision is made, the people who need to execute it are already owners of it. This is what separates value builders from people who manage consensus.

What the week produces

The pattern behind
the challenge.

The surf industry is the laboratory precisely because what it built—and what it failed to protect—is unusually legible. The operators, coaches, and shapers under the most pressure are not failing at surfing. They built their value on culture, community, and the experience of the ocean, while the forces that determine whether those things last drifted without anyone measuring them. The environmental commons degraded. The cultural identity was commodified faster than it could be renewed. The next generation arrived on entirely different terms. The financial pressure appeared last—a symptom of everything that had already shifted. The week in the Algarve is designed to make that pattern legible. Not because you work in surf. But because the same pattern—building confidently on things that are hard to price and easy to deplete—appears in every organisation that can describe its growth but cannot describe what is being quietly consumed to produce it. By Day 4, most participants can name at least one assumption in their own organisation that belongs to the same category. For those who can, the next conversation is about whether that assumption warrants a more structured look. That is what the diagnostic is for.

The ocean doesn’t teach you about surfing. It teaches you to see the assumptions your own organisation has stopped questioning.

If that pattern sounds familiar before you’ve even attended—the conversation starts here.

In the Field

The ocean is
the classroom.

Every afternoon is structured fieldwork—in the water, at the break, in the shaping bay, at the competition venue, and in structured conversations with the surf industry’s practitioners. The methodology is applied, not illustrated.

You didn’t come to the Algarve to learn about surf. You came because this is the industry where you can finally see the pattern clearly. Get Out is the first Think Wrong practice. This is what it looks like at scale.
Daily surf sessions—all levels

Be Bold made physical—every session facilitated by professional coaches, every level participates, every session debriefed using Think Wrong frameworks. The discipline of committing to a line under genuine uncertainty —and learning from what happens—is the one that transfers directly to high-stakes decisions under incomplete information.

Board shaping bay

Make Stuff inside a shaper’s workshop—the craft of building a physical object that embeds specific assumptions about who will use it, how, and in what conditions. The prototype-as-hypothesis discipline you develop here is the one you apply to your next product, service, or process decision.

Competition observation

Certainty Map and Deflection Point applied at a live surf event—separating what the industry knows about its own competitive future from what it assumes, with access to the coaches, judges, and athletes who hold and test those assumptions in real time. The same diagnostic goes home to your own competitive landscape.

Environmental fieldwork

Super Vital Assumption work with the organisations monitoring what the surf industry depends on and rarely measures—ocean health, swell patterns, coastal access, and regulatory risk. The discipline of identifying the assumption your own organisation has stopped checking is among the most transferable skills of the week.

Surf camp and school visits

Bet Small applied to two contrasting business models built on different assumptions about what a surf customer values—a high-volume beginner school and a premium adult retreat. The practice of comparing assumption sets across competing models goes home to your own portfolio decisions.

Stakeholder panel

Small bets presented to surf industry stakeholders for structured feedback—Make Stuff and Bet Small in practice, with a coach, a brand, a travel operator, an environmentalist, and the next generation of surfers at the table. The panel format is the one you run with your own stakeholders the week after the programme.

Who Should Attend

For anyone responsible for results.

Think Wrong is for anyone who owns an outcome—a revenue target, a product launch, a market entry, or a strategic initiative—and wants to be more systematic and more physical about developing the practice. You don’t have to be the most senior person in the organisation. You have to be someone responsible for making something happen, with a genuine appetite to do it differently.

You should attend if

  • You are responsible for building value in your organisation—through revenue, new products, market expansion, or strategic change—and you want a more rigorous and more embodied practice for doing it well
  • You lead or own growth initiatives and want a more rigorous practice for testing the assumptions underneath them before they consume significant capital
  • You want a methodology experience that is physical and embodied—the surf environment makes the discipline visceral in a way no classroom can replicate
  • You are comfortable being a beginner in a new domain and learning from what the conditions produce
  • You want to work on a genuinely uncertain industry challenge with real practitioners who are living the problem
  • You want to develop the facilitation capability to run assumption-testing discipline inside your own organisation after the week
  • You are making or overseeing a significant investment—in AI deployment, in growth strategy, in transformation, or in any domain where the wrong assumptions will be expensive—and you want a structured practice for testing them before the investment locks in.

You should consider waiting if

  • You are not prepared to be physically active in and around the ocean for half of each day
  • You have a medical condition that prevents participation in low-impact water activities
  • You want a programme where you can observe and theorise rather than participate and test—the ocean does not allow for spectators
  • You need a curriculum with predetermined right answers—this programme produces tested hypotheses, physical signal, and transferable capability
Every assumption you carry into the water gets tested by conditions you did not choose. That is the discipline. The ocean was the laboratory. Your organisation is where it compounds.
