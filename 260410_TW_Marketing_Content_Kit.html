Wine
Aix-en-Provence, France  ·  June 22–26, 2026
Anchor Article  Publish on LinkedIn + solvenext.com

The assumption that built the wine category is the assumption that now threatens it.

The status quo in the wine industry is not a management problem. It is a neuroscience problem. The synaptic shortcuts that allow experienced vignerons to make decisions quickly—the same efficiency that built the category—also make the assumptions underneath those decisions invisible. Biology and culture conspire together: the predictable path always feels like the rational one. The wine industry is not failing because it lacks talented people or good wine. It is operating on assumptions that growth has confirmed so many times they stopped feeling like assumptions at all.

The wine industry has a problem it cannot see clearly from the inside. Not a market problem, not a distribution problem, not a generational problem—though it uses all three frames to describe what is happening. The actual problem is an assumption that has been so thoroughly confirmed by decades of growth that it stopped feeling like an assumption at all.

That assumption is this: quality, correctly communicated, will find its buyer. That what a wine is—its terroir, its heritage, its complexity—is sufficient, if the story is told well enough, to command attention and price. The entire apparatus of the wine industry—its classification systems, its critical infrastructure, its marketing language—is built on this belief. And for most of the twentieth century, it was correct.

It is less correct now. Not because quality has stopped mattering—it hasn’t—but because the category has fragmented faster than the belief system has updated. The person who bought Burgundy at thirty is not the person who is thirty today. The sommelier whose expertise once guided a purchase is increasingly absent from the decision. The retail shelf that told a story through placement is competing with an algorithm that tells a different story through convenience.

The wine industry’s response to this has been almost entirely focused on better execution of the original assumption. Better storytelling. Better design. Better digital presence. More authentic vignerons, more compelling labels, more immersive experiences. All of it, done well. None of it questioning whether the thing being communicated more effectively is the right thing to communicate at all.

This is not a wine industry problem. It is the pattern that appears in every category that has been growing confidently for long enough that the assumptions underneath the growth stopped being examined. The beliefs that made the category work became facts. The facts became invisible. And then something changed—not catastrophically, but structurally—and the category found itself solving the wrong version of its own problem with increasing sophistication.

The most important innovation question is not “what should we do differently?” It is “which of the beliefs we are building on have we never actually tested?” In the wine industry, those untested beliefs are now visible—but only from the outside. Which is precisely why an industry at a visible deflection point makes such an effective laboratory for a discipline that most organisations need to apply to themselves.

Because assumptions are impossible to see inside the system that made them—and Provence in June is a remarkable place to develop the practice of seeing them clearly.

The arts crisis is not about audiences. It is about assumptions.

The performing arts do not have an audience problem. They have a status quo problem. The assumptions that the institutions built on—about who comes, why they come, and what the relationship between the art and the audience is—were formed in a different era and have been confirmed by enough decades of operation that they stopped being examined. The brain’s efficiency works against organisations as much as it works against individuals: what has always been done becomes what is right to do, not because it has been tested against alternatives, but because it has never had to be.

The performing arts have been telling themselves a particular story about what is happening. The story involves streaming, distraction, shrinking attention spans, government funding cuts, and the death of a particular kind of cultural seriousness. All of these forces are real. None of them is the actual problem.

The actual problem is an assumption that the institutions built on—that artistic excellence, properly presented and sufficiently accessible, will always find its audience. That the work itself, if it is genuinely good, constitutes a sufficient offer. That the question “who is this for?” was settled when the institution was founded and does not need to be reopened.

The arts crisis is not a quality problem. The work being made in Paris’s theatres, opera houses, and galleries is extraordinary. It is an assumption problem—specifically, the assumption that what made these institutions culturally essential in 1970 is the same thing that makes them culturally essential now. That the relationship between the art and the audience is fixed, that the terms of engagement are given, that the work of the institution is to maintain the standard rather than reexamine the premise.

This assumption is visible from the outside in ways it cannot be from the inside. Standing in a half-empty theatre watching a flawless production, a visiting executive from a technology company can see immediately what an artistic director who has spent thirty years in the building cannot: the programme is magnificent, but the question of who is supposed to be here, and why they should come, has not been asked for a long time.

The most useful thing an external perspective provides is not fresh ideas. It is the ability to see which questions are no longer being asked. The arts crisis is a perfect mirror for this. Every assumption the performing arts are operating on—about audiences, about price, about what counts as a compelling experience, about the relationship between heritage and relevance—is visible from the outside and invisible from within.

Which is why Paris’s arts institutions make such an unusually effective laboratory for the discipline of assumption-testing. The patterns surfaced in a conversation backstage at an opera or in a gallery conversation with a collector are structural. They appear in identical form in the pharmaceutical company navigating the shift from blockbuster drugs to personalised medicine, in the financial services firm whose client proposition was designed for a customer who no longer exists, in the technology company that built its entire business model on a user behaviour that is quietly changing.

The Think Wrong Arts Intensive uses Paris and the performing arts as the medium for developing that discipline—not because the arts are the point, but because they are the clearest available mirror right now for a pattern most organisations cannot see in themselves.

Surf
Algarve, Portugal  ·  Spring/Autumn 2026
Anchor Article  Publish on LinkedIn + solvenext.com

The surf industry built a global market on three beliefs about authenticity. All three are under pressure simultaneously.

The surf industry built something remarkable on a set of genuine beliefs. Authenticity. Community. A real relationship with risk and the natural world. These were not invented for marketing purposes—they were true, and the category grew because they were. The status quo in the surf industry is not the usual kind: it is a status quo of genuine values that have calcified into untested assumptions. Biology and culture conspire to protect even the most cherished beliefs: the things that have worked for longest are the hardest to examine, because examining them feels like betrayal rather than inquiry.

The surf industry built one of the most successful lifestyle categories in the history of consumer markets on a set of beliefs that felt less like marketing and more like a religion. Authenticity. Community. The ocean as teacher. The surfer as someone who has chosen a different relationship with risk, with time, with the natural world. These were not invented beliefs—they were genuine. The category grew because the beliefs were true, and because they were shared.

What happens when the category grows large enough that the beliefs start to conflict with the economics? When the authentic brands get acquired by conglomerates managing portfolios? When the community becomes a demographic that advertising agencies write briefs about? When the ocean is so overcrowded that the relationship with it that the industry was built on is no longer available to the people the industry is selling to?

This is the surf industry’s actual challenge. Not a product problem. Not a marketing problem. An assumption problem—specifically, the assumption that authenticity is a stable foundation for a category rather than a resource that can be depleted by the same growth it enables.

The surf industry is not alone in this. Every category built on a genuine cultural belief—craft beer, organic food, independent music, sustainable fashion—eventually confronts the same tension: the growth that validated the belief also changed the conditions that made the belief true. The belief does not automatically update when the conditions change. It persists as an assumption, shaping strategy, until something forces it to be examined.

The discipline of surfing—reading conditions, committing to a line under genuine uncertainty, learning from what the water tells you rather than what you expected—is the most physical version of the practice we are trying to develop. Not the lifestyle. Not the brand. The actual cognitive practice of committing to a decision on incomplete information, executing it, and accurately assessing what happened.

That practice, applied to the surf industry’s real challenges in the Algarve for four and a half days, produces a capability that transfers back to wherever the pressure is greatest in the participant’s own organisation. The ocean is not the point. The discipline is the point. The ocean just makes it impossible to forget.

Irish Whiskey
Wild Atlantic Way, Ireland  ·  Autumn 2026
Anchor Article  Publish on LinkedIn + solvenext.com

Forty distilleries. One assumption. The fastest-growing spirits category is making the same confident bet.

The Irish whiskey category has a status quo problem that growth has been concealing. Forty distilleries have entered on the same confident belief—that the market is premiumising, that craft and heritage are what consumers are choosing, that quality will find its buyer. The belief was true for the first entrants. It has been confirmed by growth for everyone who followed. And confirmation is not the same as testing. The synaptic shortcuts that allow an industry to act quickly on shared beliefs also prevent it from asking whether those beliefs are still sound. The absence of counter-evidence is not evidence of absence. It is just what the predictable path looks like before the turn.

Irish whiskey has grown at double digits for a decade. More than forty new distilleries have opened in the last fifteen years. Capital has flowed in from strategic investors, private equity, and heritage brands recognising a category moment. By every visible metric, Irish whiskey is one of the great success stories in global drinks.

Here is the question nobody inside the category is asking clearly: What if most of that capital is betting on the same assumption—and that assumption is wrong?

The assumption is premium positioning. Every new distillery has entered the market believing that the category’s growth is driven by premiumisation—by the consumer’s appetite for provenance, craft, heritage, and complexity. That the Irish whiskey drinker is making a considered choice based on substance and story. This belief is confirmed by the category’s most prominent success stories. Teeling. Slane. Roe & Co. The craft narrative works.

But forty distilleries cannot all occupy the premium position in the same market. When every entrant believes quality and heritage will differentiate, differentiation disappears. The premium space becomes crowded. The consumer, faced with forty craft narratives of roughly equal quality, makes the decision on different criteria than the producers assumed—price, accessibility, what the bar already stocks, what their phone recommends.

This is what assumption inflation looks like in real time. An entire category making capital commitments on a shared belief that growth has never forced anyone to test. The belief was true for the first ten entrants. It becomes less true with each subsequent one. But because the category is still growing, nobody has to confront the assumption. Until they do.

The discipline required here is not better marketing or smarter distribution. It is the prior discipline: classifying what the category knows from what it assumes, and designing the smallest possible test that would produce genuine evidence before the next capital commitment is made.

The Wild Atlantic Way is the laboratory where we develop that discipline. The Irish whiskey industry provides the live case. The assumption-testing practice is what goes home.

Automotive
Coventry & West Midlands, UK  ·  Autumn 2026
Anchor Article  Publish on LinkedIn + solvenext.com

Four forces arrived simultaneously at UK automotive. It is solving three of them incorrectly.

The UK automotive industry is solving four simultaneous disruptions with increasing sophistication and institutional courage. The status quo is not the problem—or rather, it is not the obvious problem. The obvious problem is the disruption itself: the EV transition, Chinese entrants, supply chain friction, identity crisis. The status quo problem is harder to see because it is inside the response. The brain’s efficiency—the synaptic shortcuts that produce quick, expert judgment—also ensures that the frame used to diagnose each challenge is the frame inherited from the era before the challenge existed. The industry is answering the wrong version of each question with increasing precision.

The UK automotive industry is navigating four simultaneous disruptions: the electric vehicle transition, Chinese competitive entrants, post-Brexit supply chain friction, and an identity crisis made public by the Jaguar rebrand. It is responding to all four with increasing sophistication. It is solving three of them incorrectly.

The EV transition is being framed as an engineering problem. The real challenge is an organisational identity problem: whether a company that has spent a century being exceptionally good at hardware can become a company that is exceptionally good at software. The engineering is solvable. The assumption that solving the engineering solves the transformation is the problem.

The Chinese entrant challenge is being framed as a quality and price problem. BYD and NIO did not arrive with better cars at lower prices. They arrived with different assumptions about what a car is for, who drives it, and what the purchase decision is based on. Competing on the original frame—premium engineering, heritage, driving experience—is a coherent strategy only if the new entrants are playing the same game. They are not.

The supply chain challenge is being framed as an efficiency and resilience problem. The real challenge is an assumption problem: just-in-time manufacturing was designed for a world where predictability was available. Improving the efficiency of a system built on an assumption that no longer holds produces a more efficient system built on a wrong assumption.

The Jaguar rebrand—the one that attracted the most public commentary—is actually the least wrong-framed of the four responses. It is an explicit attempt to reopen the question “who is this brand for now?” That question should have been asked of all four challenges simultaneously. It was asked of one, and only under the pressure of an identity crisis that made avoidance impossible.

The pattern here is not unique to automotive. It is the pattern that appears in every capital-intensive sector navigating simultaneous technology transition, new competitive entrants, and regulatory change. The organisation improves its execution of the current frame. The frame itself is not examined. The improvements compound. The wrong question is answered with increasing precision.

Coventry and the West Midlands—the manufacturing heartland navigating the same transition Detroit navigated, with less time and different constraints—is the laboratory where we develop the practice of reframing before the capital commits.

Every successful system eventually does this. The brain’s efficiency locks in what has worked; culture builds institutions to protect it; and the predictable path from the last transition becomes the starting assumption for the next one—unchallenged, because the last one worked.

Amsterdam removed the car from the centre of its urban mobility system before any other major European city. The decision was made in the 1970s, executed over decades, and is now so thoroughly embedded in the city’s infrastructure and culture that visiting urban planners from every continent study it as the reference case for post-car cities. It is, by any measure, a remarkable achievement.

Here is what the reference case does not show you: Amsterdam is now wrestling with the next set of assumptions. This is the European mobility transition’s deepest challenge: not the first transition (away from the car, largely achieved or underway) but the second transition, where the assumptions of the first solution are now the constraints.
